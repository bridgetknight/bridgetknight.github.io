Final render with compositing — Blender, Cycles

physically-based black hole simulation

Blender · Maya · Cycles · 2025

This project is an independent study in physically-based astrophysical visualization — an attempt to make a black hole that doesn't just look dramatic, but behaves correctly. The gravitational lensing shader is derived from Schwarzschild optics, the accretion disk texturing follows known thermal gradient patterns, and every aesthetic decision was made in service of scientific accuracy. The goal was a render that a physicist would recognize, not just an audience.

Final Render

The compositing pass adds atmospheric glow, a subtle starry background, and lens flares, pushing the render toward the kind of image NASA's Event Horizon Telescope produces, after some touch-up, rather than a purely synthetic look. The difference between the two renders shows how much the post-processing contributes to legibility--the raw Cycles output is accurate but flat; the composite is accurate and tangible.

Blender Development

The gravitational lensing effect is built entirely in Blender's node editor--a Schwarzschild displacement shader that bends light paths around a central mass using vector math nodes to approximate relativistic light deflection. The accretion disk uses a procedural noise-based texture with temperature-mapped emission, hotter near the inner edge and cooler at the outer rim, consistent with standard accretion disk thermodynamics. Volumetric scattering along the disk plane adds physical density to what would otherwise read as a flat texture.

Process & Exploration

Early in development I explored building the simulation in Maya using MASH networks for the accretion disk geometry and Arnold for rendering. The material development showed promise but Maya's node-based shading system offered less flexibility for the custom light-bending logic the lensing effect required. Blender's more accessible shader node editor and Cycles' volume rendering capabilities made it the better tool for this specific problem. The Maya exploration wasn't wasted, though, instead it clarified exactly what the Blender implementation needed to do differently.
